The Evolution of the Movie¶

Do they make 'em like they used to?¶

It's often said that films are getting longer, and possibly even more commonly said that they have gotten worse over the years. After all, the golden age of Hollywood ended about sixty years ago.

Using data science techniques, we will explore how much of that is fact and how much is fiction...

by William Gerst - CMSC320 - 17 May 2021¶

Introduction¶

Let's look at the total film runtimes of some of the most popular movies throughout time, according to the American Film Institute's 100 Years... 100 Movies collection of classic films.

Back in 1927, we see The Jazz Singer runs for about 88 minutes in total.

The 1939 Frank Capra classic Mr. Smith Goes to Washington clocks in at 129 minutes.

Then, in 1976, Robert Redford's hit All the President's Men hits 138 minutes in total.

The most recent entry on the list is 2001's fantasy film The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring which reaches an astounding 178 minute runtime.

Inspecting only this short list of movies suggests that perhaps there is some kernel of truth to the claim that movies have been getting longer. However, it's not too hard to find counterexamples to this trend, even on that same list from the AFI. For instance, Gone with the Wind from 1939 achieves a runtime of 238 minutes, higher than any of the four others listed above.

Clearly, no number of anecdotal examples will be sufficient to finally prove or disprove this idea that movies runtimes have been increasing. To do that, we will instead perform large-scale data analysis on the set of all movies with pages on the Internet Movie Database, or IMDb.

Before moving on, one more observation to make is that the user ratings for the four films above tended to increase with time. In sequence, they received IMDb scores of 6.5, 8.1, 8.0, and 8.8 out of 10, respectively. In the analysis that follows, we will investigate to see if this pattern holds up in general, not just for those special few on the aforementioned AFI list.

Gathering the Data¶

IMDb provides a multitude of different datasets described on their website. These datasets contain information ranging from basic facts like the titles of movies and TV shows to the specific job titles of principal cast and crew members for particular works.

For our purposes, all we need are the files title.basics.tsv.gz, which contains the titles, dates, and runtimes of films, and title.ratings.tsv.gz, which (as expected) holds the average user ratings for each work.

These files can be directly downloaded from https://datasets.imdbws.com/ and extracted to make the files basics.tsv and ratings.tsv, respectively. These files, which together comprise about 700 MB, can be placed in the directory imdbData/.

After this, we can use Pandas to read in the files directly into DataFrames for easy processing. Pandas is a user-friendly data management package for Python which allows quick and easy data storage and manipulation in a 2D table format, similar in concept to SQL databases.

import numpy as np

# Returns the average of a series of numbers
def avg(ls):
    return sum(ls) / len(ls)

averageMovieData = pd.DataFrame(columns=["year", "frequency", "averageRating", "averageRuntime"])

# Make a list of all years that appear in the IMDb data
years = set(y for y in movieData["startYear"] if y != "\\N" and int(y) < 2022)

# Keep a list of dictionaries for aggregate movie data by year
yearDatas = []

# Make an entry for every year that appears in the data
for yr in sorted(years):
    moviesByYear = movieData[movieData["startYear"] == yr]
    freq = len(moviesByYear)
    
    # We take the average of all movies with user ratings, if any
    yearRatings = moviesByYear["averageRating"].dropna()
    if not yearRatings.empty:
        averageRating = avg(yearRatings)
    else:
        averageRating = np.nan
    
    # Now, we take the average of all movies with listed runtimes, if any
    runtimes = [int(r) for r in moviesByYear["runtimeMinutes"] if r != "\\N"]
    if runtimes:
        averageRuntime = avg(runtimes)
    else:
        averageRuntime = np.nan
    
    yearData = {
        "year": int(yr),
        "frequency": freq,
        "averageRating": averageRating,
        "averageRuntime": averageRuntime
    }
    
    yearDatas.append(yearData)

# Create a DataFrame for storing aggregate movie data by year
averageMovieData = pd.DataFrame(yearDatas, columns=["year", "frequency", "averageRating", "averageRuntime"])

Now, we see that for every year between 1915 and 2021, with the odd exception of 1920, the average IMDb rating is contained entirely within the fairly small range of 5.8 to 6.4 out of 10 stars. There are no obvious linear or polynomial trends in rating, unfortunately, though we see that the period from 1970 to 2000 generally has lower ratings than the time ranges that come before and after. One plausible explanation for this is that IMDb users are split between casual demographics that like more modern, post-2000 films and others (potentially critics) that have a taste (or feeling of nostalgia) for older classics. After all, in the updated 2007 version of the American Film Institute's list of 100 movies mentioned previously, only twelve were released in 1990 or later.

Conversely, there is most definitely a pattern that appears in the runtime versus year plot. Namely, from 1915 onwards, the runtime appears to increase steadily from about 60 minutes on average to between 90 and 95 by the year 1960. At that point, it remains roughly constant, perhaps even decreasing a bit to 85 minutes around the year 2010.

Further Evaluation¶

In the previous section, we explored to try and find if any interesting relationships occurred between the variables of interest in our data. In particular, we tried the following:

  1. Quantity of movies versus year
  2. Average IMDb rating versus year
  3. Average runtime versus year
  4. IMDb rating versus runtime

To summarize, we observed that the quantity of movies increased dramatically over time (1), especially past the year 2000. There was very clearly a nonlinear relationship to this increase, and there were in fact several features of the data that were directly attributable to current and past historical events.

The average rating versus year (2) and rating versus runtime (4) plots, on the other hand, clearly exhibited no useful trends upon visual inspection, and any tools or techniques like polynomial regressions that suggest otherwise would simply be misleading.

The average runtime versus year data (3), though, certainly did show an interesting pattern. To wit, the average film runtime rose from about 60 minutes in 1915 to between 90 and 95 minutes in the 1960s and beyond. After about 1960, the average runtime fluctuated up and down in small amounts, but never went outside the range of about 85 to 95 minutes. before falling back down to approximately 85 minutes by 2010. Prior to about 1955, though, the scatterplot looked to be roughly linear in shape. Thus, it stands to reason that we should perform a linear regression on the data in this range to check if it is truly a good fit.

We are investigating the dependence of average film runtime on year, so our null hypothesis $H_0$ is the claim that runtime is independent of year. In terms of variables, if our linear regression yields the equation $\beta_0+\beta_1x$, then this is equivalent to saying $\beta_1=0$. We set our significance level at $\alpha=0.05$, since this is the industry standard for non-critical applications. (There is no noteworthy danger to individuals or to society that can come from a type I error here, so there is no particular need for us to designate a lower value of $\alpha$.)

For finding a linear regression to plot on top of our scatterplot, we use numpy's built-in polynomial fit function, which simply requires input data, output data, and a degree for the polynomial. Then, to evaluate the fitness of this model, we use the statsmodels package with the ordinary least-squares (OLS) analysis to produce a summary of relevant statistical quantities.

From the output above, we see that the linear regression yields the equation $\text{Runtime}\approx64.3+0.753\text{(Year - 1915)}$, where runtime is measured in minutes. The graph of the curve on top of the scatterplot from before certainly appears to be a very good fit for the data from a cursory inspection. From the statsmodels summary, though, we can infer a few more detailed numerical properties of the model and its fitness. This post and this post each provide good explanations for the different quantities in the above table and how to interpret them properly.

From the $R^2$ value, we see that the linear fit based on release year accounts for roughly 94.3% of the variability in the average film runtime from 1915 to 1955.

The P-value for the slope of the regression line is rounded to 0.000, which means it is far less than the significance level of 0.05 that was chosen previously. We can even see that the 95% confidence interval for the value of the slope was given by $[0.692, 0.814]$. Thus, the chance that film running time was independent of year in the span from 1915 to 1955 is vanishingly small.

Some of the values at the very end of the table help us to check whether the proper assumptions for a linear regression are met.

The Omnibus and Jarque-Bera tests both check the normalcy of the residuals, and the associated probabilities tell the probability that the residuals are in fact normally distributed. These values of 0.707 and 0.749 are quite high, indicating that this is a fairly good assumption.

To check if residual variances are constant (homoscedasticity), we can look at the Durbin-Watson value. Ideally, this would lie between 1 and 2. Unfortunately, in this instance, the regression produced a value of 0.718, which is suboptimal and may indicate that variances are not constant. In context, this could make sense, as the film industry matured and runtimes became more standardized over time.

The different values for runtime (and the resulting residuals) are certainly independent from one another by nature, since the runtime of one movie does not directly impact that of another. Finally, there definitely does appear to be a fundamentally linear relationship between runtime and year within this specific date range, which is likely due to gradual experimentation by filmmakers and adjusting of public expectations.

Conclusion¶

At the beginning, we set out to learn how movies have changed over time. More specifically, we wanted to find answers to two of the most common questions on the subject:

  • Have movies been getting better/worse?
  • Have movies been getting longer?

In our preceding exploration and analysis, we have found satisfactory answers to each of these questions.

To address the first point, there was not sufficient evidence from IMDb user reviews to conclude that movie quality has changed significantly over the course of filmmaking's relatively brief history. In almost every year since the end of World War I, the average rating by year has fallen in the range of 5.8 to 6.4 stars out of a total of ten.

Though the IMDb ratings have not changed much, this is not definitive proof that movie quality has stagnated. For example, it could very well be the case that, as conjectured before, certain demographics tend to regard films from certain time periods more highly than others just by nature, or that older films' ratings are artificially inflated by nostalgia.

As for whether movies have been getting longer, we have more solid evidence from which we can draw conclusions. Namely, we found that while average movie runtime has remained about constant between 85 and 95 minutes from about 1955 to the present day, this has not always been the case. Instead, average movie runtime increased approximately linearly with time between the years 1915 and 1955, at a rate of about 0.75 additional minutes every year.

This change in runtime was likely due to maturation of the movie industry as filmmakers and directors attempted to find the right balance between the minimum time needed for good storytelling and the maximum time for which they could maintain the interest of a general audience. Coincidentally, this transition from increasing to roughly constant runtime also happens to align with the end of the golden age and the transition from black-and-white to color in Hollywood, another sign that the industry was developing.

In short, the next time you hear someone complain about how movies are so long nowadays, unless they're old enough to remember black-and-white films in theaters, they're probably looking back through rose-tinted glasses.
